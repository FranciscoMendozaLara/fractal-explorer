The Mathematics Behind the Explorer

Order hidden in infinite recursion

Every spiral, fractal, and surface you see is governed by a single irrational number that has fascinated mathematicians, artists, and architects for millennia.

01

The Golden Ratio

Defining φ

The golden ratio φ is the unique positive number satisfying a deceptively simple self-referential property: the ratio of the whole to the larger part equals the ratio of the larger part to the smaller.

Definition
$$\frac{a+b}{a} = \frac{a}{b} = \varphi$$

Substituting $b = 1$ and $a = \varphi$ yields the characteristic equation:

Characteristic equation
$$\varphi^2 = \varphi + 1 \quad \Longrightarrow \quad \varphi = \frac{1 + \sqrt{5}}{2} \approx 1.6180339887\ldots$$

This number is irrational and in fact the most irrational number in a precise sense — it is the hardest real number to approximate well by rationals, which is why spirals built on φ pack objects most efficiently without interference patterns.

02

Fibonacci Sequence

The discrete road to φ

The Fibonacci sequence is defined by $F_n = F_{n-1} + F_{n-2}$ with seeds $F_0 = 0,\ F_1 = 1$. The ratio of consecutive terms converges to φ.

Binet's closed form
$$F_n = \frac{\varphi^n - \psi^n}{\sqrt{5}}, \quad \psi = \frac{1-\sqrt{5}}{2}$$
Convergence
$$\lim_{n \to \infty} \frac{F_{n+1}}{F_n} = \varphi$$

03

The Logarithmic Spiral

Geometry of self-similarity

A logarithmic spiral grows outward at a constant angle to any radial line. The golden spiral is the special case where the radius grows by exactly φ after each quarter-turn.

Polar form
$$r(\theta) = a \cdot e^{b\theta}, \qquad b = \frac{2\ln\varphi}{\pi} \approx 0.3063\ldots$$

The explorer renders this relationship directly — the φ ratio slider controls the base of the exponential, morphing the spiral away from the golden ideal.

Implementation
$$r_i = R \cdot \varphi^{-\ell} \cdot \varphi^{\,f \cdot T - T}, \quad f = \frac{i}{N},\quad \ell = \text{layer}$$
04

Julia Sets

Complex iteration and escape-time dynamics

For a fixed complex constant $c$, the Julia set is the boundary between starting points $z_0$ whose orbits under $z \mapsto z^2 + c$ remain bounded, and those that escape to infinity. The explorer scans every pixel as a starting point and colors by escape speed — the true escape-time algorithm.

Iteration & smooth coloring
$$z_{n+1} = z_n^2 + c, \qquad \nu = k - \frac{\ln\ln|z_k|}{\ln 2}$$

Click anywhere on the canvas to move the constant $c$, watching the fractal's shape deform continuously. Points near the boundary of the Mandelbrot set produce the most intricate Julia sets.

05

The Torus & Golden Distribution

φ on a curved surface

A torus with major radius $R$ and minor radius $r$ is parametrized by two angles $(\theta, \phi)$. Points are distributed using the golden angle — the irrational rotation that avoids clustering — so the surface is filled as uniformly as possible.

Torus parametrization
$$\mathbf{x}(\theta,\phi) = \begin{pmatrix}(R + r\cos\phi)\cos\theta \\ (R + r\cos\phi)\sin\theta \\ r\sin\phi\end{pmatrix}$$
Golden angle distribution
$$\theta_k = 2\pi k / \varphi^2, \quad \phi_k = 2\pi k / \varphi$$

The torus is then projected onto the 2D canvas using a rotation matrix, giving the illusion of 3D depth with hidden-surface depth cueing.

06

Solid Donut Rendering

3D shading on a curved surface

The Donut mode renders the torus as a proper solid 3D object using the painter's algorithm — the surface is tessellated into small quadrilateral faces, each shaded with a Blinn–Phong lighting model that combines ambient, diffuse, and specular components.

Blinn–Phong shading
$$I = k_a + k_d\,(\hat{n}\cdot\hat{L}) + k_s\,(\hat{n}\cdot\hat{h})^{\,\alpha}$$ $$\hat{h} = \frac{\hat{L}+\hat{V}}{|\hat{L}+\hat{V}|} \quad \text{(halfway vector)}$$

Each face's surface normal $\hat{n}$ is computed analytically from the torus parametrization, giving smooth shading across the surface. The colour hue cycles around the ring (the $\phi$ angle), so the donut reads as a gradient wrapping the tube.

07

Lissajous Figures

Harmonic composition in the plane

Lissajous curves arise from two perpendicular sinusoidal oscillations. Because the y-frequency uses $\lfloor A_\text{arms} \cdot \varphi \rfloor$, which is irrational relative to the x-frequency, the curve never perfectly closes — filling a region densely instead.

Explorer implementation
$$x = R\,\varphi^{-\ell}\sin(p\,\theta + \phi_\ell)\cdot s, \quad y = R\,\varphi^{-\ell}\sin(q_\ell\,\theta)\cdot s$$ $$q_\ell = \lfloor p\,\varphi \rfloor + \ell, \quad s = 0.5 + 0.3\,\text{curl}$$
About

Why the golden ratio?

This explorer started as a question: what does mathematics look like when you make it interactive? The golden ratio felt like the right answer — it sits at the intersection of number theory, geometry, biology, and art.

Every parameter has a direct mathematical meaning. Moving a slider is moving a variable in a live equation. The torus + Julia mode in particular connects topology, complex analysis, and fractal geometry in one image.
